PDF documents are everywhere since 'PDF' lives up to its name: Portable Digital Format. It can combine raster and vector data in a while preserving the publisher's layout in a cartesian system1. PFS may be easy on the eye, but to machines they are much less tractable. Unlike HTML, selecting words, images, and blocks of text in PDFs is based on spatial coordinates and some embedded "fragment identifier" bookmarks. Still, identifying and linking to portions of PDFs is possibe.

Sometime between 2005-2007, Adobe introduced "PDF Open Parameters" to allow users (machine and otherwise) to identify and link to sections of PDFs. The general syntax is [URL]#parameter_1=value¶meter2=list,values. The best-known and most widely implemented pdf open parameter is #page=num. You may not have known you may specify zoom=percent,x,y where x and y are coordinates from the top-left of the page, or highlight=x0,y0,x1,y1 describing a rectangle to highlight on open. Adobe lists even more open parameters and a method for combining multiple parameters with ampersands. However, few of these open parameters aside from page and zoom are implemented in the browser. Mozilla, is working to implement pdf open parameters, but the features seem to have been on the back burner since 2014.

That's why I built pdf-hardlinkr, which you can try out here. It uses a svg overlay on top of PDF.js's canvas to highlight a urlencoded point.

This a nonstandard feature and so uses nonstandard urlencoding syntax, but in the future I hope to implement further standard PDF Open parameters in a more complete version of the PDF.js viewer.

1Since PDF relies on cartesian coordinates, you might be able to hack together GIS tools to display and query data in pdfs.
